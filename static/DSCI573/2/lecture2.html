How to aggregate the error distances?

  • Mean squared error (MSE) \[ MSE = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} ({y_i - \hat{y}_i})^2 \]
from sklearn.metrics import mean_squared_error
mean_squared_error(Y, Y_pred)
1623684888.2162929
  • A large number.
  • What is a square dollar?

Root mean squared error (RMSE)

\[ RMSE = \sqrt{MSE} = \sqrt{\frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} ({y_i - \hat{y}_i})^2} \]

np.sqrt(mean_squared_error(Y, Y_pred))
40294.97348573756
  • Error of $40k dollars is more interpretable than 1 billion square dollars.

RMSE most common

Mean absolute error (MAE)

\[ MAE = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} |{y_i - \hat{y}_i}| \]

from sklearn.metrics import mean_absolute_error
mean_absolute_error(Y, Y_pred)
32334.243006758294
  • Interpretability.
  • More robust to outliers.

How will these errors change with shift?

  • They will all remain the same.
  • However, the right model would be considered better.

  • They will all remain the same because they are measuring a absolute or squared distance.

  • This remains the same regardless of the offset of the model.

  • However, the rightmost model would be considered more performant because the total price is more. The error is a smaller proportion of the total price.

Mean absolute percentage error (MAPE)

\[ MAPE = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} \frac{|{y_i - \hat{y}_i}|}{y_i} \]

  • Error now relative the size of the actual values

  • Easier to compare different models

  • Easy to understand and communicate

  • However, there are caveats like that MAPE is not defined for division by 0, often solved by adding smal lvalues, but…

  • and the score can get huge for small errors on actual values that are very close to zero (since we are dividing with such a small number)

  • And it tends to favor underforecasting, so be careful if predicting something sensitive to underforecasting

  • Suppose our forecast is F_t=2, then an actual of A_t=1 will contribute APE𝑡=100% to the MAPE, but an actual of 𝐴𝑡=3 will contribute APE𝑡=33%

\(\small R^2(y, \hat{y}) = 1 - \frac{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \hat{y_i})^2}{\sum_{i=1}^n (y_i - \bar{y})^2}\)

  • The proportion of variation in \(Y\) that can be explained using \(X\).

  • The default scoring metric in sklearn!

  • Depending on how you define 𝑅2 , it can be equivalent to minimizing the sum of squared residuals. While this minimization is famous from OLS linear regression, nothing about such a minimization is special for linear models, and it is fine to minimize the sum of squared residuals in other regression models. It might even be fair to say it is common to minimize the sum of squared residuals for regression problems.

  • Note that maximizing this value is equivalent to minimizing the numerator, which is exactly the sum of squares residuals. (The denominator is a constant that depends on the data, not the model. You get the same denominator whether you fit a linear model, a gradient boosting model, or a deep learning neural network.)

  • An advantage of is that it gives a context to the sum of squared errors by comparing to the performance of a baseline “must beat” model. A drawback of 𝑅2 is that it lacks the natural units that are given by the squared errors, which can be interpreted. In your example, the developers evidently value the former in their model evaluation.

Inspecting the error of the model

  • Residual analysis: plot the error vs. the predicted value or features.

  • Regardless of which error metric you pick, it is important to visualize your predictions to see what type of error you are doing. Does the model generally over or under predict and does it seem to struggle with some specific type of predictions.

  • In this type of plot, the ideal case of zero error is a flat line instead of a diagonal one, which can make it easier to see patterns in the data.

  • Centered around zero: The model’s predictions are unbiased.

  • Randomly distributed: No systematic patterns remain.

  • Constant variance (homoscedasticity): The spread of residuals should not vary with predicted values.

  • Random Scatter: The residuals appear to be randomly scattered around the horizontal line at zero. This suggests that the model’s predictions do not show systematic bias and the assumption of linearity might be reasonable.

  • Potential Outliers: There are some points with relatively large residuals (far from zero), indicating potential outliers in the data or areas where the model may not perform well.

Exercise 2: Choosing Metrics

  • Predicting sales in dollars, where large errors are particularly costly.
  • Predicting stock prices, where both under- and over-predictions are equally important.
  • Forecasting demand as a percentage of historical values, where relative errors are more critical than absolute errors.
  1. Mean Squared Error (MSE) or Root Mean Squared Error (RMSE).
  2. Mean Absolute Error (MAE) or RMSE.
  • Stock price predictions often involve occasional extreme changes due to market events, which may lead to significant outliers in the prediction errors.
  1. Mean Absolute Percentage Error (MAPE).

Ridge regression

High level idea

Why Use Ridge Regression?

  • Addresses multicollinearity.




  • Bias and variance trade-off (details in lecture 6).

When predictors are highly correlated, ordinary least squares (OLS) estimates can become unstable. Ridge regression stabilizes the coefficients by shrinking them.

Ridge regression introduces a small bias (due to coefficient shrinkage), but this often reduces variance significantly, improving the model’s overall performance on test data.

By adding the penalty term, Ridge regression limits the size of coefficients, which reduces the model’s variance and makes it less sensitive to noise.

Brier score - MSE for classification

  • Logistic regression gives probability, then we use a hard threshold to make decisions.
  • Predictions like p=0.49 and p=0.1 for y=1 contribute the same error?

How wrong a single classification is?

  • \[ \text{Brier Score} = \frac{1}{n} \sum_{i=1}^{n} ({\hat p_i - y_i})^2 \]
  • \[0 \leq \text{Brier Score} \leq 1\]

Brier score - p_i is the probability that was forecast, - y_i the actual outcome of the event (0 or 1)
